Early City Economy

How terrain, city centers, and your capital shape the first age of a settlement.

Prototype note: this page documents the intended early city economy model. Pieces will arrive in the playable build as the prototype grows—use it as a compass, not a checklist of what is on screen today.

Core idea

Cities do not simply produce abstract progress because they exist. A city draws value from the tile it is founded on, the land its people work, special buildings or institutions, and—when fate smiles—capital status and the seat that represents it.

Yields in the early prototype

Four basic yields describe what a place contributes:

Yield Role
Food Supports growth and survival.
Production Builds units, buildings, and projects.
Science Advances what your people are learning and researching now.
Coin An early, flexible economic yield. In the ancient era it may stand for tribute, stored valuables, prestige exchanged between kin-groups, or surplus gathered and guarded. Later ages may read the same underlying yield as coinage, tax, currency, finance, or something stranger—without changing the spine of the rules.

Behind the scenes the game can keep a stable internal name for this yield while the words you hear in later eras shift to match the world’s story.

Worked land (intended v0)

When people work a tile directly, these are the baseline yields the land is meant to offer in the earliest slice. (Science does not normally come from raw terrain here.)

Terrain Food Production Coin
Grassland (flat) 2 0 0
Plains (flat) 1 1 0
Grassland hills 1 1 0
Plains hills 0 2 0
Grassland woods 1 1 0
Plains woods 1 2 0
  • Grassland tilts toward feeding many mouths.
  • Plains balance field and workshop.
  • Hills ask more effort from the soil but reward sweat with production.
  • Woods offer timber, forage, and craft—another bump to practical output.
  • Coin is not expected from untouched ground in this first table.

City center: a settled home tile

A founded city is not merely the same numbers as an empty field someone walked across yesterday. The first circled hearths, granaries, paths, and shared labor make the city center more reliable than ordinary worked land.

Intended normalization (applied to the home tile when it becomes a city):

  • Food = the greater of the tile’s Food or 2
  • Production = the greater of the tile’s Production or 1
  • Coin = 0 from this rule alone
  • Science = 0 from this rule alone

Examples (city center only, before other buildings):

Founding tile Raw tile City center
Grassland flat 2 Food / 0 Production 2 Food / 1 Production
Plains flat 1 Food / 1 Production 2 Food / 1 Production
Grassland hills 1 Food / 1 Production 2 Food / 1 Production
Plains hills 0 Food / 2 Production 2 Food / 2 Production
Plains woods 1 Food / 2 Production 2 Food / 2 Production

A clever founding site still matters: a production-rich heartland tile keeps its higher production after the walls go up—you are not punished for choosing strong ground.

Capital and Palace

The first city you raise for your people is your capital. It begins with a Palace—not only a hall of rulers, but the early hearth of authority: memory kept in song and tally, messengers on the trail, storehouses under watch, ritual that binds kin to season, and the slow negotiation of who owes what to whom.

That institution is why the youngest empire can already organize research and administration before scattered towns sprout their own laboratories.

Palace (intended v0 bonus on top of the city center):

  • +1 Science
  • +1 Coin

Production still rises first from terrain and the settled center; the Palace leans toward learning, record, and the first pooled surplus—not the saw and the forge by themselves.

Capital examples (city center + Palace):

  • Grassland capital: center 2 Food / 1 Production; Palace +1 Science / +1 Coin.
  • Plains hills capital: center 2 Food / 2 Production; Palace +1 Science / +1 Coin.

How science begins

Early science should feel earned from institutions and learned order, not as an automatic echo from every new dot on the map. At the start, the Palace gives the baseline pulse of inquiry and record. Later, science may flow from buildings, specialists, hard-won discoveries, rare worked grounds, dedicated research efforts, and systems yet to be named.

New cities should strengthen your realm through food, production, territory, and the promise of what they will become—not, on turn one, as a second full research engine duplicating the capital’s role.

Why this matters later

This foundation is the quiet root under many branches to come:

  • Population working the land around a city
  • City territory and the ring of tiles you may claim
  • Workers who reshape the countryside
  • Farms, mines, quarries, lumber camps, and other improvements
  • Improvements that rewrite what a tile gives
  • Paths such as Stone Tools unlocking workers and mine or quarry craft
  • Deeper capital bonuses and ordinary buildings
  • Specialists and focused roles within the city

Empire of Minds is not only about climbing a fixed technology tree. Each world can shape what knowledge matters—but where and how you settle still whispers the first lesson.
